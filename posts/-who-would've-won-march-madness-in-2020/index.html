The Blueprint of a Champion: Who Would Have Won the 2020 Men’s March Madness Tournament?

On March 12, 2020, the NCAA made the unprecedented decision to cancel the men’s tournament due to the spread of COVID-19. Just like that, March Madness was gone, and for the first time, there was no national champion. A season that had built up so much excitement with players like Dayton’s Obi Toppin and the nation’s leading scorer, Markus Howard; Cinderella teams that had a real shot like Dayton and San Diego State; and then the blue bloods like Kansas, Duke, and Villanova, we ended with no real conclusion to this season. A lot of teams had strong cases, but the question remains: Who would’ve actually won the 2020 NCAA men’s basketball tournament? To answer that, I started with every single Division I team from the 2020 season. From there, I used the Simple Rating System (SRS), a metric that measures how good teams actually are based on the strength of schedule and scoring margin. The average team is 0, so if you’re a +5, that means you would beat the average team by 5; if you’re a -5, then you would lose to an average team by 5. This metric is better than looking at just wins and losses because it measures how much you win by, as well as how good a team you are playing.

After narrowing the field a little bit, the next step was figuring out what a national champion actually looks like. To do that, I took the last 20 national champions (2000-2019) and compared their offensive and defensive efficiency ratings. Offensive efficiency measures how many points a team scores per 100 possessions, and defensive efficiency measures how many points a team allows per 100 possessions. So you want to have a high rating for offensive efficiency and a low rating for defensive efficiency. Every single champion had a balance of great offense and great defense; champions don’t just rely on one end of the floor, but they can dominate on both sides.

Now obviously, there can only be one champion, so I took the remaining 111 teams and evaluated them on their offensive and defensive efficiency ratings. I used the minimum champion offensive rating (113.4, 2014 UConn) and the maximum champion defensive rating (95.0, 2018 Villanova) to filter out which were the actual “champion contender” teams.

From there, I got a little more analytical to filter out my remaining 11 teams. Every national champion since 2003 has ranked in the top 12 of the week 6 AP poll. With this metric, this eliminates San Diego State (25), Villanova (20), Michigan State (16), and Dayton (14), so now we have 7 remaining teams: Kansas, Gonzaga, Baylor, Duke, Ohio State, Louisville, and Maryland. To separate these final contenders, I used net rating. A net rating combines a team’s defensive and offensive efficiency into one number by subtracting the defensive rating from the offensive rating. The higher the number, the more dominant and complete a team is. Every national champion in the last 20 years has had a net rating of at least 23.28.

After narrowing the field down to 4 teams, it was time to come up with a conclusion. Each team showed traits of past champions. Gonzaga had the best offense in the entire country, but a below-average defense and a weak strength of schedule. Baylor and Duke had great balance all around, with Baylor’s defense having a similar rating to 2019 Virginia. However, Kansas stands out with the best Net Rating in the country (30.23), the best record (28-3), the toughest strength of schedule, an elite offense and defense, and the nation’s highest SRS of 24.96. Based on historical stats and trends as well as comparisons to past champions, the Kansas Jayhawks would be your 2020 men’s basketball national champions.
